5 Steps To Get Your Van On The Road

Before getting started on your conversion, it’s best to know how to get it legally on the road. When you begin your research, you'll find that in Ireland the process can be a little bit confusing, and the required information is a bit all over the place. Here is a step by step guide to help you get clear on what needs to be done, and in what order.

The first thing to know is that to get your camper insured, it first must be registered as a motor caravan on the logbook. In the eyes of Revenue, for a self-converted van to be considered a motor caravan, it must have the following:

  • Seating and a table
  • A bed (this can be converted from the seats)
  • Cooking facilities
  • Storage facilities

When it comes to cooking facilities and gas, Revenue state that a Gas Installer Certificate should be made available. In my experience, this is not the case. That said, when installing gas, it is recommended that you use a registered gas installer. Having your gas installed by an RGI will give you peace of mind, and the certificate will be of benefit should you ever be selling your camper in the future.

Below I have set out five key steps to getting your van on the road, one before you start your build and four once you’ve completed your build. This article does not go into specifics on the build itself. You can find information on how to convert your van here.



Pre Build

Step 1: Speak to the insurance provider.

You may be thinking, why would I be doing this before I even start my conversion? The reason is, in Ireland, as mentioned above, there are only basic standardized requirements set out by Revenue to convert a commercial vehicle into a camper. Most insurance providers have additional requirements relating to different areas of your build, particularly gas installation. It’s a good idea to speak to the insurance provider before getting started, avoiding any nasty surprises when it comes time to get the van insured. In Ireland, you have three options, Dolmen Insurances, Stuart Insurances, and the Motor Caravan Club of Ireland.



Post Build

Step 2: Get your van weighed.

Before your van can be inspected by an SQI (step three), you will need to find a weighbridge in your local area and have your van weighed. Your best bet is to try a local recycling centre. I used Marrakesh Recycling Centre in Kilmacanogue, Co. Wicklow, costing €20.


Step 3: Have your van inspected.

It’s now time to get an SQI (suitably qualified individual) to certify your conversion. In this case, it will be an automotive engineer. The SQI will inspect the vehicle, make sure the conversion is up to spec and provide the necessary declaration. The SQI will also take photos of the interior and exterior of your van. These will need to be submitted to Revenue along with your declaration. The cost of the inspection is in the ballpark of €200.

I would highly recommend using Noel Maher from ATECEA. He is a great person to deal with and has a wealth of knowledge and experience in certifying conversions.


Step 4: Submit your documents to Revenue.

Good news, we are almost there! It is time to submit all of your documents to Revenue. These includes:

  • Vehicle Registration Certificate
  • SQI - Declaration of Conversion
  • Copy of the weigh docket
  • Conversion photographs
  • Cover letter and conversion receipts

Your cover letter should briefly outline how you went about your conversion and the associated cost. I have created a cover letter template that you can download here.

All of your documents should be posted to:

FREEPOST
The Central Vehicle Office
Revenue Commissioners
Anne Street
Co. Wexford
Y35 E29K


An important note...

Based on your application, Revenue will determine the open market value of your newly converted camper. You will then be subject to VRT on that value of 13.3%. This will need to be paid before your camper can be officially registered and insured.


Step 5: Insuring your van.

Your DIY conversion is officially a camper! You can now go about insuring. As mentioned earlier, you have three options, Dolmen Insurances, Stuart Insurances, and the Motor Caravan Club of Ireland. Each insurer has different terms and conditions; I would recommend taking a look at each and deciding on a provider that suits your needs best.

And that’s it! As I said, when looking online, this information tends to be a bit all over the place. I hope that you now have a clear understanding of the process and what’s involved. Should you have any questions, feel free to get in touch.
